Aperture Equivalence

Given an Aperture is described by an f-number, this completely describes the aperture effects on Exposure, no matter the sensor size of the camera. However, different sensor sizes will affect the Field of View and Depth of Field.

When comparing a camera with a regular sensor (say a full frame DSLR) and another with with a smaller sensor (say a smartphone), this makes it impossible to define a perfect equivalence between Aperture f-numbers. The same number will yield the same exposure but different

The same can be applied to Aperture, as the relationship between sensor size and aperture is very similar to that between focal length and sensor size. It’s important to remember that it’s the physical size of the aperture that defines Depth of Field, not its f-number. The f-number is the ratio between the Focal Length and the entrance pupil, so smaller cameras will have smaller apertures for the same f-number, and therefore greater Depth of Field.

Equivalence is a way of comparing different sensors on a common basis. This is already the way most lenses are talked about: it’s quite common to say that a compact camera includes a ‘28-120mm lens’ while the key and (often unspoken) word in that description is ’equivalent.’ In the digital era, the significance of sensor sizes isn’t always appreciated, not helped by a rather opaque naming system.

A 100 mm equivalent Focal Length on a small-sensor camera will give the same framing and the same perspective as an actual 100mm lens does on a regular full-frame camera.

This means that f-number alone isn’t enough as a descriptor to compare apertures for cameras of different size.

Two lenses offering the same angle-of-view with 25mm diameter apertures will give the same depth-of-field of shot at the same shooting distance and when viewed at the same output size.
